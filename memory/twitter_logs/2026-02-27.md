> 📝 Samsung's Source of HBM4 Confidence: '1c DRAM'… Next Goal Is Yield Improvement

Samsung Electronics is showing strong confidence in the competition for the 6th-generation HBM4 (High Bandwidth Memory) market, a core memory component for AI industry infrastructure. The company is being assessed as having most optimally achieved the top-tier performance demanded by its largest customer, NVIDIA, by employing a more advanced process node compared to competitors for HBM's key technological elements.

In particular, Samsung Electronics made the decisive move to enlarge the die size of its core die, the 1c (6th-generation 10nm-class) DRAM. A larger die size can simultaneously improve the stability of both DRAM and HBM4.

However, this decision works unfavorably from a profitability standpoint, as it reduces the number of chips producible per wafer. Additionally, with 1c DRAM yields still hovering around 60%, opinions have emerged that Samsung Electronics must accelerate process maturation as quickly as possible.

According to industry sources on the 27th, Samsung Electronics has recently been focusing on yield improvement for 1c DRAM alongside preemptive mass production shipments of HBM4 for NVIDIA.

HBM4 is the next-generation HBM expected to enter full-scale commercialization this year. It will be adopted in earnest for NVIDIA's latest AI accelerator, the 'Rubin' chip, with a significant performance boost through a doubling of I/O (input/output) pins to 2,048 compared to the previous generation.

NVIDIA in particular has continuously requested memory suppliers to raise the performance bar required for HBM4. While the original HBM4 performance standard set by JEDEC (Joint Electron Device Engineering Council) was at the 8Gbps level, memory suppliers have recently elevated this to 11.7Gbps for testing with NVIDIA.

Samsung's Source of HBM4 Confidence, 1c DRAM… "Enlarged Die Size for Stabilization"

Although the formal qualification test schedule has not been finalized, Samsung Electronics internally shows strong confidence in HBM4 commercialization. A prime example is its preemptive HBM4 mass production shipment ceremony held on the 12th.

At the time, Samsung Electronics emphasized: "From the initial stage of HBM4 development, we set performance targets exceeding JEDEC standards," and "We secured stable yields and industry-leading performance from the very start of mass production without any redesign."

The foundation of this confidence lies in the cutting-edge process applied to HBM4. Samsung Electronics adopted 1c DRAM—one generation ahead of competitors—for the HBM4 core die. It also mass-produced the base (logic) die, which serves as HBM's controller, using its own foundry's 4-nanometer (nm) process. This is a considerably more advanced process compared to SK hynix, which uses TSMC's 12nm process.

Initially, the industry expressed considerable concern over Samsung's aggressive technology bet. Compared to SK hynix and Micron, which use the same core die (1b DRAM) as in HBM3E for their HBM4, yield stability could be significantly worse. Indeed, Samsung Electronics experienced yield shortfalls during the early development stage of 1c DRAM.

Samsung Electronics' breakthrough was enlarging the 1c DRAM die size. Around the end of 2024, Samsung decided to modify part of the 1c DRAM design. The key was to maintain the critical circuit linewidths while partially relaxing peripheral circuit linewidth standards to reduce manufacturing difficulty.

Synthesizing accounts from inside and outside Samsung Electronics, the 1c DRAM die size enlargement achieved two major effects. First, improved 1c DRAM yields. As peripheral circuit implementation became easier than before, Samsung's 1c DRAM yields have been improving at a reasonably solid pace. Industry estimates put Samsung's 1c DRAM yields for HBM4 at 50–60% as of this month.

Second, the enlarged die size is assessed to have secured stability in the TSV (Through-Silicon Via) process, which is essential for HBM manufacturing. HBM4 requires more TSV holes to be drilled into the DRAM due to the increased number of I/Os compared to previous generations. Samsung's 1c DRAM has a larger available area, allowing TSVs to be placed with relatively more room, which eases TSV density and facilitates thermal management and reliability.

An industry official explained: "We understand Samsung Electronics has put in place multiple safeguards, including the 1c DRAM die size enlargement, for the timely commercialization of HBM4. As a result, assessments of HBM4 are favorable both internally and from the customer's perspective."

Profitability Somewhat Disadvantaged… Yield Improvement Needed

However, assessments also suggest that Samsung's HBM4 profitability falls short relative to competitors. Typically, as DRAM processes advance, die sizes shrink, enabling higher output from the same wafer. But Samsung's HBM4 1c DRAM has a larger die size than originally planned, which is unfavorable for profitability.

Yields also inevitably lag behind competitors who use the same core die as HBM3E at this stage. On top of that, yields structurally decline when applying the packaging process (TC-NCF: Thermo-Compression Non-Conductive Film) that stacks and connects each DRAM layer.

The cost of the 4nm process Samsung applied for HBM4 is also more expensive than TSMC's 12nm process. An industry official noted: "Both the core die and base die costs applied to Samsung's HBM4 are higher than competitors, so rapidly improving yields is the key issue."

Macquarie Securities also noted in a recent report: "Samsung Electronics' HBM operating profit margin is below 50%, lower than NAND, due to unfavorable deal terms, low production yields, and small production scale," adding that "Samsung needs to improve HBM margins through further price increases and improvements in yield and scale."
